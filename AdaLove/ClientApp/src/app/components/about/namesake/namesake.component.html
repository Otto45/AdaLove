Computer scientist aspirants, meet Mrs. Augusta Ada King, Countess of Lovelace, aka "Ada Lovelace!" Why have we chosen Mrs. Lovelace to be our namesake you may ask? Well, buckle up and let us teach you some brief history! Now hold up, I know what you may be thinking right about now. This lady has an old-timey portrait, did they even have electricity when she was alive?! Let us explain. You see, before Nikola Tesla and Thomas Edison showed us all how to efficiently harness the power of Zeus, Ada Lovelace was very into math. After meeting a man named Charles Babbage, he was so impressed by her mad math skills that he invited her to check out his difference engine.

Ada Lovelace
The Difference Engine, recreated by The London Science Museum

As you can see to the left, the difference engine was an actual machine. What does it do? Simple. It creates a table of answers for sequencial inputs to a function. Huh? It's a calculator! You set it up for a function, and it gives you an answer table.

So, Ada being the math lover she was, became good friends with Mr. Babbage. Later on, Babbage designed a new device he dubbed the "Analytical Engine." This device can be described in today's terms as "Turing-complete," meaning it could do arithmetic, had integrated memory, and incorporated control flow by being able to do conditional branching and looping. All of this was done mechanically. Ready for the goosebumps? Charles Babbage designed the world's first computer!

Plans for The Analytical Engine
The world's first computer program

How does this have anything to do with our girl Ada? Get ready for more goosebumps. You see, general purpose computers don't do one explicit task, they have to be given instructions on what to run at any given time. One might call these instructions a "program." Now Ada and Charles being the good friends that they were, Babbage allowed Ada to review his plans for his new computer. Not only did Ada figure out how it worked, but she devised and published a set of instructions to run on it. In other words, Ada Lovelace wrote the world's first computer program! And to us, that makes her the mother of computer science.
